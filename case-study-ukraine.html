The Situation

A sector in crisis, an organization ready to respond

When Russia invaded Ukraine in February 2022, early childhood educators and caregivers across Ukraine and refugee-hosting countries faced immediate, unfamiliar challenges. They were supporting children while themselves navigating disruption, fear, and grief.

ISSA had the networks and expertise to help — but not yet the communications infrastructure to deliver support quickly and at scale. Within weeks, the organization mobilized. I led the communications work that enabled the response.

The Challenge

Reach and speed, in a context that allowed neither

The problem wasn't a lack of knowledge — ISSA's member network included specialists in trauma-informed practice, Psychological First Aid, and early childhood pedagogy across dozens of countries. The problem was reach and speed.

How do you get the right resources to the right people, in the right languages, and in a form they can use — especially when the situation on the ground is changing daily and the people you're trying to reach are themselves in crisis?

The Approach

Building the infrastructure while deploying it

Standing up the platform

The first communications priority was infrastructure. With no dedicated portal in place, we developed an online training platform from scratch — scoping requirements, coordinating with web developers, and overseeing content architecture to ensure the platform could host multilingual resources, support self-paced learning, and scale as the response grew.

This wasn't a polished, long-planned product launch. It was built under pressure, in real time, with a small team and an urgent mandate. Getting it live and functional required a different approach than a regular web portal build. I was tasked with proposing what was essential, what could wait, and what had to be cut entirely to keep things moving.

Developing and translating materials

In parallel, I oversaw the team providing technical support for multilingual trainings, managing translation of psychosocial and educational resources, and coordinating with partner organizations to ensure timely dissemination across the network.

Impact

  • Nearly 200 trainers reached across 21 countries — from refugee-hosting nations in Europe to frontline contexts closer to the conflict
  • Multilingual resource library developed and maintained, with materials adapted for diverse regional contexts
  • Online resource portal built from the ground up

What This Required

Building through uncertainty

This work demanded rapid decision-making with incomplete information, emotional resilience given the subject matter, and a communications discipline rooted in usefulness above all else. Materials and messaging needed to be not just accurate, but genuinely helpful for educators and caregivers working under extraordinary strain.
